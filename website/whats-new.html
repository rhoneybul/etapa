Weekly check-in Experimental

Five quick questions, every week — and your coach reshapes the next one

The biggest change in this update. At a time you choose, your coach pings you for a structured weekly check-in: a small set of questions about how the week actually went and what's coming up. You answer on the phone, the coach reads it, and proposes concrete adjustments to next week — which you can apply or skip individually. It's the bit that turns a static plan into a live, adaptive one.

This is experimental. Especially the injury / physio loop — that's the newest, most opinionated part. The coach's medical guardrails are strict, but a non-medical AI is the wrong tool for anything serious, so please treat the suggestions as conversation starters, not prescriptions. We're shipping it to learn — feedback on what was useful, what was off, and what the coach got wrong is the most valuable thing you can send us right now. Use the feedback button in Settings, or reply to your coach.

The questions

Five, all designed to be answerable in two minutes:

  • Which sessions did you do? — multi-select pulled from this week's plan. Tick a session, optionally drop a one-line note ("felt strong, hit the targets" / "bonked at km 60, ran out of fuel"). The coach reads the notes, not just the boxes.
  • Anything you'd like to change about the plan? — free text. Feedback the coach can act on next week.
  • Anything coming up that affects training? — travelling Wed–Fri, family commitments Saturday morning, work crunch — whatever's going to bump rides.
  • Any injuries or pain? — Yes / No, then a short description if Yes.
  • Will you see a physio? — only when injury = Yes. If you tick yes, we add a placeholder physio appointment to your plan so it doesn't slip.

A line we won't cross

Etapa is a training app. Your coach will never give medical advice. No diagnoses, no rest-period prescriptions for medical conditions, no exercises for healing, no medication suggestions. The system prompt enforces this; the in-form banner reminds you of it the moment you tick "Yes" to injury; and if any pain is reported, the only medical-side response from the coach is "please see a physiotherapist." We'll dial down next week's load while you're getting assessed, then adapt to whatever the physio says — but we won't pretend to be one.

The physio loop

If you opt in, a placeholder physio appointment lands on your plan in the next few days. Once you've been, you can paste the physio's notes back into the app from that activity — and the coach re-runs the suggestions filtered through the notes. So a "lay off heavy lateral movement for two weeks" line from your physio becomes specific session changes in your plan, with no further input from you needed.

Coach has memory now

Each check-in feeds the coach the last three weeks' responses too — so patterns get spotted. A recurring knee niggle gets escalated, not restarted from zero. A repeated Sunday bonk gets called out by name in the summary so you can see we're tracking it. Same life event derailing the same day every week? The schedule adapts, not just the load. And when you've genuinely improved — moved from missing two sessions a week to hitting all of them — the coach acknowledges it, briefly, no gushing.

When and how it lands

You pick the schedule in Settings → Weekly check-in: day of week, time of day, with the device's timezone resolved automatically. Default Sunday 18:00, opt-in only. At your scheduled time you get a push notification. If you don't respond:

  • +12 hours — gentle reminder push.
  • +24 hours — second push, slightly more direct.
  • +48 hours — next time you open the app, an in-app popup nudges you. (Once. Then it's just the home banner until you act on it.)
  • +14 days — the check-in expires silently. We don't pile up unanswered ones.

A pending check-in shows up as a pink banner on Home between your coach card and the week module — tap to open, × to dismiss without answering. If life happened and you've got two unanswered, we only ever show the most recent — no guilt-piling.

The suggestions step

After you submit, the coach takes 10–20 seconds to read everything and writes back: a 1–2 sentence summary of what they noticed, then a list of concrete changes for next week — modify, skip, or switch-to-recovery. Each change has its own Apply / Skip button so you stay in control. Apply commits straight to the plan. Skip leaves it alone. Done when you're done.

Privacy: the responses live on Etapa's server and are sent to Anthropic's Claude API to generate the suggestions, same as every other coach-AI interaction in the app. The medical guardrails apply server-side too — we explicitly tell Claude it's not a medical professional and to refer injuries to a physio. Nothing about your responses is shared with anyone other than you and the coach.

Indoor sessions Experimental

Get your indoor session into Zwift, Wahoo SYSTM, Rouvy or TrainerRoad

Open any indoor session and you'll see a "Send to your trainer" button above your coach. Tap it and we generate a real structured workout file — warm-up, intervals, cool-down, with power targets as a percentage of your FTP so the trainer drives resistance in ERG mode. The file is exactly the shape the indoor app would have asked you to build by hand.

This is experimental. The file generator works and produces valid .zwo / .mrc — every receiving app accepts the file. Where it gets messy is the handoff: trainer apps don't accept .zwo via the iOS / Android share sheet, so the realistic workflow is a web upload (per-app instructions below). Tell us how you used it — which path actually worked, where the friction was, what felt obvious or weird — and we'll prioritise the next round of integrations from there. A direct TrainingPeaks bridge and native .fit are both candidates; we want to ship the one that removes the most friction for actual riders.

Two formats:

  • .zwo — the modern standard. Read by Zwift, Wahoo SYSTM, Rouvy, MyWhoosh, TrainerRoad, intervals.icu, and most others.
  • .mrc — universal text fallback for older software (CompuTrainer / PerfPro / ErgVideo / GoldenCheetah).

How the file actually gets into your trainer app

To be straight with you: trainer apps on iOS and Android don't accept .zwo files via the share sheet. We tested it. "Copy to Zwift" sometimes appears in the share sheet but doesn't reliably import. The honest workflow is one of two paths.

Path A — intervals.icu sync bridge (fastest, mobile-only)

intervals.icu is free, takes .zwo uploads on mobile, and has built-in sync to Zwift, Garmin Edge, and Wahoo ELEMNT. Connect it to Zwift once (intervals.icu → Settings → Zwift) and any workout you upload to intervals.icu appears inside Zwift's Custom Workouts on next launch. Same for Garmin and Wahoo head units. This is the only path that works phone-only without a laptop.

  1. In Etapa, tap "Send to your trainer" → ".zwo".
  2. Pick "Save to Files" (iOS) or save to Downloads (Android).
  3. Open intervals.icu in your phone browser → sign in → Workouts → Calendar → drag the .zwo onto a date.
  4. Workout appears in Zwift / Garmin / Wahoo on next launch.

Path B — laptop-based web import (per app)

If you'd rather upload directly to your trainer app's account: save the .zwo, then sign in to the app's web dashboard from a laptop and import. Workouts sync to the mobile app on next launch.

  • Zwift — only via the desktop client. Save the .zwo, then on a Windows / Mac drop the file into Documents/Zwift/Workouts/{your-id}/. It appears under Custom Workouts on next Zwift launch on any device on the same account. Zwift currently has no web import.
  • Wahoo SYSTM — systm.wahoofitness.com → Workouts → Custom → Import.
  • Rouvy — my.rouvy.com → Workouts → Add → upload the .zwo.
  • TrainerRoad — trainerroad.com → Workouts → Workout Creator → Import.
  • CompuTrainer / PerfPro / older apps — choose .mrc instead of .zwo when you export.

Android specifically

Same as iOS, just clearer end-to-end. Tapping export drops the .zwo into your phone's Downloads folder. From there you have two paths:

  • Phone-only: upload to intervals.icu (free, mobile-friendly), connect intervals.icu → Zwift / Garmin / Wahoo, and the workout flows through.
  • Have a laptop: transfer the file (USB / Drive / email-to-self), then web-import via SYSTM / Rouvy / TrainerRoad, or drop into the desktop Zwift folder.

Etapa's first-time export popup walks through both paths in plain English; it has a "Don't show this again" checkbox once you've got a workflow that works for you.

A few more things worth knowing. ERG-mode resistance only works if your trainer app knows your FTP — set it once inside the app, not in Etapa, and every imported workout plays back at the right watts. Garmin Edge and Wahoo ELEMNT head units don't read .zwo directly; the intervals.icu bridge is the easiest path there too. Native Garmin Connect Workout export (.fit) and a direct TrainingPeaks bridge are next on the roadmap.

Privacy: the export URL is signed with a per-server secret and expires in five minutes. It's scoped to a single (rider, plan, session, format) tuple — it can't be re-used to fetch any other workout, and tampering with the URL (changing the activity id, the user, or the format) fails the signature check.

Multi-bike training

Pick every bike you ride — swap any session, any day

The "what kind of cycling?" question is now multi-select. Tick every bike in your shed (road, gravel, MTB, e-bike, indoor) and your plan will use them all. On any session, the BIKE row shows all five chips — tap a different one and we suggest a sensible distance and duration adjustment to keep the training stimulus the same. Same effort, same time, different kilometres. Multipliers came from a coach Q&A: gravel ≈ 0.75×, MTB ≈ 0.65×, indoor drops distance entirely, e-bike ≈ 1.05×. "Keep original" is one tap away if you'd rather hold the road numbers as a stretch goal on gravel.

For combos that just don't work — road threshold intervals onto an MTB — the sheet flips into a "this swap won't work well" mode and points you at a better choice or a reschedule. Override is still right there.

Bike chips show on every home card and every list-view row, with a chevron so you can tap-to-swap from anywhere without drilling into the activity detail.

Choosing your plan

A plan built around where you actually are — in under a minute

Three quick questions: what are you training for, how long is your current longest ride, and when's your event. That's it. No FTP test, no jargon, no "beginner / intermediate / advanced" guesswork. Your actual longest ride becomes the anchor for Week 1, so your plan starts at a distance you can genuinely complete and builds up from there.

On the training-types and build-your-week steps you'll see a small "we typically recommend" card — and the recommendation adapts to your fitness level and the session types you've already chosen. After your answers, a review screen lays out every choice as a tap-to-edit row — no scrolling back through the flow, no starting over, and going back from any later step actually goes back.

Drag and drop

Move a session straight from home or the calendar

Press-and-hold any session on Home and drag it to a new day. Or open Calendar, press-and-hold any cell that already has a ride on it, and drag the session right out of the grid — no need to scroll down to the day list to pick it up first.

Same gesture, same press-and-hold timing in both places. If you move a session to a day before Week 1 starts, we honour the move without re-generating the rest of the plan around it.

Messaging

A chat that opens instantly and never loses a reply

We rewrote how coach chat is stored end-to-end. Open the screen and your conversation is right there from the cache, then quietly reconciles with the server in the background. Send a message and close the app mid-reply — when you come back, your message and the coach's reply are both intact. If a send fails, a small inline resend arrow appears inside the bubble, exactly where you'd expect to tap.

Push notifications now drop you on the exact message that triggered them, not the top of the chat. Plan-ready taps go straight into the new plan overview. And ask "what does today's session look like?" — your coach now answers with a proper structured breakdown: warm-up, main set, cool-down, with the effort target for each block.

Home screen

Today, tomorrow, and your week — one screen, no scrolling

Your today's session is the first thing you see — name, distance, duration, effort, and a one-tap "View details" button. Tomorrow sits right beneath it. Below those, the next five days run in a compact horizontal scroll with session-type tags (Endurance, Intervals, Long Ride…) so the week reads as a real week, not two looming hero cards.

Your coach chat lives at the top of the screen so you're never more than a tap from a question. The "+ New plan" button shrunk to a small circle in the YOUR PLANS header — only the full pill version shows up when you don't have a plan yet, where it actually belongs.

Onboarding

You meet your coach the second you arrive

The onboarding tour fires the moment you finish signing in, before you see an empty home screen wondering what to do. Three steps, then you're in.

Picking a coach and choosing km or miles aren't optional any more — those two answers shape the rest of the app, so we ask once and remember forever. The Continue button stays disabled with a clear "Pick a coach to continue" hint until you've chosen.
