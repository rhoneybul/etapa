# Part 1 — The story

The distilled narrative. Don't memorise — internalise. You'll retell this a hundred times in the next year; the versions below are what it compresses down to when you need it in 10 seconds, 60 seconds, or 5 minutes.

## The core thesis (one sentence)

> **Cycling has always been gatekept. Etapa opens the gate.**

That's the whole pitch. Everything else is evidence for it.

## The one-paragraph version (café test)

If a stranger asks what Etapa is, this is the default:

> Cycling is one of the most gatekept pastimes in fitness. Every app assumes you already know the language — FTP, TSS, zone 2 — which is intimidating the moment you want to start. Etapa is different. It's an AI cycling coach that speaks in plain English, builds personalised plans around real life, and treats "first 10 km ride" as a goal every bit as worthy as "first 200 km sportive." I'm building it for beginners, for women who've been put off by cycling's culture, and for people coming back to the sport after years away. I only got into cycling because I had mates who told me what to do — Etapa is that mate, in everyone's pocket.

Memorise the **first sentence** ("Cycling is one of the most gatekept pastimes in fitness"). That becomes your automatic opener in every conversation about Etapa.

## The motivation — why you're building this

*North-star section. This is the foundation the video, blogs, and IG content all flow from. If a piece of content doesn't ladder back to something here, it probably shouldn't be made.*

- **I didn't find cycling easy myself.** Despite loving it now, the start was hard, confusing, and easy to give up on.
- **There's a long list of things no one tells you when you start:**
  - What kit you actually need (and what you can skip)
  - Where to buy it from, and what's worth spending on
  - How to find good routes near where you live
  - What to eat before, during, and after a ride
  - How to find a local cycling club
  - How to find your first event or race
  - What cycling can actually *become* in your life
  - How to go from a road ride to a bike packing trip
- **I only got through it because I had mates.** Most people don't.
- **Coming from Australia, I didn't even understand cycling as a sport.** In Australia it's how you get to the shops. In Europe it's a culture, a lifestyle, an adventure. That gap exists for a lot of people, and it's part of why getting in is so intimidating.
- **The market gap is real.** Every cycling app out there is built for people who already know what FTP means. Nothing is built for the person at the start.
- **My girlfriend getting into it showed me this first-hand.** Writing her a plan was the spark — it made me realise this should exist, and no one was building it.

## The goals — what Etapa is trying to do

- **Bring everything a beginner needs into one place.** Plan, coach, guidance on kit, fuelling, routes, clubs, events, bike packing — all under one roof.
- **Remove the intimidation.** No jargon. No assumption you already know the lingo. No feeling that you need £2k of kit before you clip in.
- **Give people a plan that actually fits their life** — not a pro training block retrofitted for beginners.
- **Give people a coach they can actually talk to.** The kind of mate I had, but in their pocket — ready to answer the "stupid" questions without judgement.
- **Help people find *their* version of cycling.** That might be:
  - Getting fit and staying fit
  - Making friends and finding community
  - A first event, a first race, a first century
  - Travelling and exploring on two wheels
  - Bike packing, disappearing into the hills, the real adventure side
- **Open the door to the adventurous side of cycling.** This is the part I personally care about most — the Munda Biddi, the Morocco rides, the long unsupported trips. I want people to know that's *available to them* too, not reserved for a tiny club of people who already know how.
- **The bigger mission:** get more people into cycling. Make it less intimidating to start. Help them stay in it long enough to fall in love with it. And then help them chase whatever version of it means something to them.

# Part 2 — The intro video (outline)

## 3. Why cycling matters to me — the thread through my life

*This is the heart of the video. Keep it grounded in concrete memories, not abstract ideas. Each sub-beat works as its own 30–45s reel.*

### 3a. Childhood — first feeling of freedom

- Riding as a kid. Bike with a surfboard strapped to it, riding to the beach.
- That was my first real feeling of freedom — being able to just go somewhere on my own.
- It's a small thing that sounds short, but it shapes you.

### 3b. My dad's Morocco ride

- The way he talks about it — as exploration, as challenge, as the thing that made him.

### 3c. First road bike at 20 — commuting era

- Riding to work, listening to tech podcasts, early in my software engineering career.
- Bike as a release valve — way to de-stress on the way home, way to wake my brain up in the morning.
- This is when it became a daily part of who I am, not just a hobby.

### 3d. Moving to the UK — discovering cycling as a sport

- I'm from Australia originally. Growing up there, cycling wasn't really "a sport" in my head.
- You rode to the shops. You rode to the beach. That was it.
- In Europe, cycling is *massive* — a proper sport, a proper culture, a proper lifestyle.
- Moved to the UK, had friends who were deep into it, and I got pulled in.
- Got into road cycling properly. Then gravel. Then events.
- Quick montage-style list (don't labour it):
  - Ironman
  - Race Across Switzerland — 500km
  - Rode to my mum's house
  - Rode to France to watch a race
  - Cycling club, trips to Mallorca and Granada
  - Most of my closest friends now — through cycling

### 3e. The bike packing piece (the real love)

- Of everything, **bike packing is the one.**
- Loop back to dad: this is me continuing what he started.
- The magic: get on your bike with a bit of kit, and you can genuinely survive and go anywhere.
- No other sport gives you that. Not running, not climbing, nothing.
- The one I really want to do: **the Munda Biddi in Australia** — one of the great long off-road bike trails in the world. Full circle for me.
- Something I want to be doing forever.

## 4. What cycling actually is (pushing back on the stereotype)

# Part 3 — Tone & delivery notes

For you as you shoot it.

- **Pauses are good.** Don't rush the childhood and dad stories — those are the emotional spine.
- **Avoid jargon.** No FTP, no zone 2, no threshold talk in this video. This one is about the *why*, not the *how*.
- **"Mates" and "mum" — keep them in.** That's your voice. Don't sand it off.
- **Don't sell.** Mention the app, land the tagline, move on. The story is the sell.
- **Repeatable phrases to lean on** (these will become reusable lines across blogs + IG):
  - "first feeling of freedom"
  - "the bike is the unlock"
  - "for the person at the start line, not the podium"
  - "cycling has always been gatekept"
  - "start riding, we'll handle the rest"
  - "I didn't find it easy either"
  - "everything I had to piece together from mates, in one place"

## Things NOT to do on camera

Hard no's, from `BRAND.md` and the voice guide:

- **No "I'm excited to announce"** or any variant.
- **No "we're going to revolutionise cycling."** We're not. We're making it more welcoming.
- **No crushing, beasting, grinding, unlocking, supercharging.**
- **No "for all levels of cyclist."** We're specifically NOT for everyone — say who it's for (beginners, women, returning) and who it's not (racers, power-meter users).
- **No apologising.** "Sorry, just a quick one" / "bear with me while I explain." Confident, calm, unhurried.
- **No pitch deck energy.** This is a story you're telling a friend over coffee, not a round announcement.
- **No "I'll keep this brief."** You won't — it's 10 minutes — and saying so makes people think you should have.

# Part 4 — Shoot logistics (pre-flight)

## Specs

- **Length:** 8-15 minutes. Longer is fine. **Do not shoot a 90-second marketing clip.** YouTube rewards depth, and this video has a job beyond views — it's the source of truth for every downstream marketing asset.
- **Camera:** Your Mac's FaceTime camera is genuinely fine. No DSLR. No ring light.
- **Audio:** AirPods are OK but a wired lav mic or even wired EarPods with the microphone are better. Audio > video quality, every time.
- **Setting:** Somewhere that tells a bit of the story. Your desk with a bike visible in the background is great. Not a sterile white wall.
- **Take:** One continuous take. No cuts. Edit only to trim the ends, not to splice mid-sentence.
- **Time budget:** 90 min total — 30 min to review this doc + sticky note, 15 min to set up, 15 min recording + re-recording if you flub badly, 30 min to trim in CapCut.

If any answer is "no", don't shoot yet:

- [ ] Have I read Parts 1-3 at least twice?
