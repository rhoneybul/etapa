# Etapa — Brand Guidelines

---

## Brand Overview

Etapa is an AI cycling coach built for people who don't yet see themselves as cyclists. While every competitor assumes you've arrived — with power meters, race schedules, and training jargon in hand — Etapa starts from where you actually are. The name says it: etapa means stage, a single leg of a longer journey. Every ride is a stage. You don't have to see the whole route to start.

The core promise is a knowledgeable coach in your pocket, available any time, that speaks to you like a person — not a performance metric. Where TrainerRoad and TrainingPeaks build for the already committed, Etapa is the door in. It is built for beginners, for women who have been put off by cycling's exclusionary culture, and for anyone who wants to improve without it feeling like a second job. Plans that fit real life. Language that doesn't exclude. Science that stays in the background. AI that genuinely adapts to you — not the other way around.

Cycling has always been gatekept. Etapa opens the gate.

## Target Audience

### Overview

Etapa's audience is defined not by what they've already achieved on a bike, but by where they want to go. They are curious, motivated, and often put off by the culture and complexity that dominates the cycling world. They don't need a lighter carbon frame or a lactate test — they need someone to explain what to do, in plain English, without making them feel like an outsider. Etapa has three distinct audience segments, each with its own needs, barriers, and relationship with the sport.

---

### Primary Audience: The Beginner

**Who they are**

The Beginner is someone who wants to get into cycling — or wants cycling to mean more than an occasional weekend loop — but has no structured training background. They may have bought a bike recently, or have one gathering dust. They've tried downloading an app before and bounced off it within a week because it assumed knowledge they didn't have. They follow cyclists on Instagram and think it looks achievable, but feel out of their depth the moment they encounter terms like FTP, TSS, or Zone 2.

They're typically between 25 and 45. They work full-time. They have commitments — family, social life, inconsistent energy levels — that make a rigid training plan feel unrealistic. They are not looking to race. They're looking for progress that feels real: being able to ride further than they could last month, keeping up on a group ride without dying, completing a charity sportive they've signed up for out of optimism.

**What motivates them**

The Beginner is motivated by visible, tangible progress. Not watts or power curves — the feeling of a ride that felt easy last month feeling harder because they've pushed further. They want to feel like they're doing it right without needing a sports science degree to understand why. They want to complete something they set out to do, and feel proud of it.

They're also motivated by habit. Most beginners know the hardest part isn't any individual ride — it's showing up consistently. An app that helps them build a sustainable routine, rather than burning them out in week two, is what they're looking for. They want to get to a point where cycling is something they just do, not something they have to psych themselves up for.

**What holds them back**

The primary barrier is intimidation — not physical, but cultural. The cycling world has a language, an aesthetic, and a set of assumptions that tell beginners they're not quite ready yet. Apps ask for FTP on day one. Forums assume you know your cadence. YouTube tutorials reference equipment most beginners don't own. The unspoken message everywhere is: come back when you're more serious.

The secondary barrier is complexity. Most cycling training apps present a wall of data — TSS, CTL, ATL, form score — before the user has completed a single session. For a beginner, this creates paralysis, not motivation. They don't know what to optimise, so they optimise nothing and eventually quit.

The third barrier is pricing anxiety. At £15–20 a month, committing to a cycling app feels like a big bet for someone who isn't sure they'll stick with it. The risk of paying and not using it is more off-putting than the cost itself.

**How Etapa serves them**

Etapa removes all three barriers. There is no jargon in the onboarding. Goals are framed around feelings and achievements ("ride with confidence", "complete your first sportive") rather than metrics. The AI coach is available 24/7 to answer any question without judgment — including questions that would feel embarrassing to post in a forum. Plans are built around the days and times the user can actually ride, not an idealised training week. The Starter plan (one-time payment, 3 months access) removes the subscription risk entirely.

The coach chat is particularly important for this audience. Beginners carry a disproportionate number of small, practical questions — saddle height, what to eat before a ride, whether it's normal to feel this breathless at this pace — that go unanswered because there's nowhere obvious to ask them. Etapa makes every question answerable, immediately, without the user having to expose their inexperience in a public space. That alone removes one of the biggest silent barriers to sticking with the sport.

The tone throughout is warm, clear, and honest. It says: you can do this, here's how, and there's no wrong way to start.

### Secondary Audience: Women Getting Into Cycling

**Who they are**

Women represent the fastest-growing segment of new cyclists — growing at over 15% annually — and are consistently the most underserved. The secondary audience for Etapa is women between 25 and 50 who are interested in cycling either as a fitness tool, a social activity, or a personal challenge, but who have been put off by the culture, language, and design of existing apps and communities.

They are not necessarily complete beginners. Some have been riding casually for years. What unites them is that they have never found a cycling product that felt like it was genuinely made for them. The defaults across the industry — aggressive language, male-skewed imagery, power targets calibrated for male physiology, forums that can be unwelcoming — have created a persistent feeling of being an add-on rather than a primary customer.

**What motivates them**

This audience is motivated by enjoyment, achievement, and community. They want to ride further, get fitter, and feel good doing it. They're interested in the social side of cycling — group rides, charity events, sportives — as much as solo performance. Many are drawn to cycling because it's low-impact and can be fitted into everyday life: commuting, weekend rides, time away from screens.

They respond strongly to products that take their goals seriously without being condescending or performative about inclusion. They don't want a "women's version" of a cycling app with a pink logo and softer targets. They want an app that simply doesn't make them feel like an afterthought — and that happens to have female coach voices, flexible scheduling, and language that doesn't make them feel like they need to be tougher to belong.

**What holds them back**

The primary barrier is cultural. Cycling has a well-documented problem with inclusivity. From online communities to kit design to the way apps are marketed, the default cyclist is implicitly male, experienced, and performance-focused. Women who don't match that image often disengage early, not because cycling isn't for them, but because nothing in the ecosystem is designed with them in mind.

The secondary barrier is language. Words like "beast mode", "crush it", and "no excuses" are pervasive in fitness culture and land very differently with this audience. They signal that the product is optimised for a particular kind of motivation — aggressive, ego-driven, performance-above-all — that many women actively want to avoid. The absence of that language is itself a product decision.

The tertiary barrier is scheduling. Women disproportionately carry non-negotiable commitments outside of work — childcare, caregiving, household responsibilities — that make rigid training plans unrealistic. An app that adapts when life gets in the way, without making the user feel like they've failed, is more valuable than one with a technically superior training algorithm.

**How Etapa serves them**

Etapa's design and tone are deliberately inclusive by default, not as a feature bolted on. There is no aggressive language anywhere in the product. The three most visible AI coaches are women (Clara, Sophie, Elena) — not as a token gesture, but as the natural result of designing for an audience that includes women. Plans are built around availability, not an ideal week, and the AI coach adapts when schedules change.

The onboarding asks about goals, available days, and current fitness — not about power meters or race history. The result is a plan that feels personally built, because it is. The coach chat allows users to ask questions they might not ask in a public forum, at any time, without judgment.

### Tertiary Audience: The Returning or Lapsed Rider

**Who they are**

The lapsed rider cycled regularly at some point — perhaps years ago, perhaps just last year — and has fallen out of the habit. They know more than a complete beginner and don't need hand-holding on the basics, but they've lost fitness, lost routine, or lost motivation. They might be returning after injury, a busy period at work, having children, a life change, or simply drift. The gap between where they were and where they are now feels embarrassing, even though it shouldn't.

They tend to be slightly older (30–55), have a reasonable bike already, and don't need to be convinced cycling is for them — they just need something to bring them back and keep them accountable. They're often more likely to convert to paid subscribers, more likely to engage consistently once they've started, and more likely to recommend the app to friends when it works. They represent strong long-term retention value.

**What they need from Etapa**

They need a plan that acknowledges where they are now, not where they were. They don't want to feel like they've regressed or failed — they want a clear starting point and a realistic path forward. The AI coach is particularly valuable here: it allows them to describe their situation in their own words ("I used to ride 100km a week but haven't been on the bike in a year") and get a plan that genuinely reflects that reality, rather than a generic beginner template that insults their existing knowledge or an intermediate plan that's immediately discouraging.

The flexibility of Etapa's scheduling is also critical for this group. Returning riders often have complicated lives — the same ones that caused them to stop in the first place. A plan that says "you have three available days this week and two next week, that's fine" is far more likely to survive contact with real life than one built around an assumed training block.

**What motivates them**

More than beginners, returning riders are motivated by recapturing a feeling they already know. They remember how it felt to be fit on a bike — the ease of a long ride, the satisfaction of consistent progress — and they want that back. Etapa's tone of quiet, honest encouragement resonates here. They don't need cheerleading. They need a plan that starts sensibly, a coach that adjusts when things get derailed, and the reassurance that starting again is not the same as starting over.

---

### Audience Synthesis

What unites all three segments is a single shared need: a training experience that respects where they are. Not where a race schedule assumes they should be. Not where a forum decides they ought to be. Where they actually are, right now. Etapa's job — in every screen, every session description, every line of coach chat — is to make that person feel capable, informed, and glad they started. The goal is not to produce elite cyclists. It is to produce people who ride regularly, enjoy it, and come back.

---

### Who Etapa Is Not For (Yet)

Competitive Cat 1–3 racers who need detailed power analysis, WKO-style modelling, and periodisation mapped to a race calendar are better served by TrainingPeaks or a human coach. Triathletes needing multi-sport periodisation across swimming, cycling, and running are outside the current scope. These users may find value in Etapa's AI coaching chat, but the core product is not optimised for them and should not be marketed to them at this stage. Building for this audience would compromise the beginner-first design principle that is Etapa's primary competitive advantage.

## Tone

Five principles that define how Etapa speaks:

1. **"Plain English, always."** — No jargon unless it's been explained. "Ride at a pace where you can hold a conversation" beats "Zone 2 aerobic effort." If a first-time user wouldn't understand a word, replace it.
2. **"Like a knowledgeable friend, not a performance coach."** — Warm, direct, and honest. Gets to the point. Never patronising, never preachy. Doesn't pad with corporate filler phrases.
3. **"Celebrate the small wins. Mean it."** — Completing week three of a twelve-week plan deserves acknowledgement. Not in a gamified, badge-dispensing way — in a human "that actually matters" way.
4. **"No judgment. No excuses. No intimidation."** — Questions are never stupid. Missing a session isn't a failure. The tone never implies the user should be doing more, working harder, or caring less about rest.
5. **"Science-backed. Human-voiced."** — The training methodology is grounded in real sports science, but that science stays in the background. It backs up the plan — it doesn't need to be on display.

## Style

Five principles that define how Etapa looks and feels:

1. **"Dark, focused, distraction-free."** — Near-black backgrounds (`#000`, `#0a0a0a`) keep the focus on the content. Premium without being cold. The interface steps back so the plan steps forward.
2. **"Pink that says energy, not aggression."** — The accent colour (`#E8458B`) is warm magenta — energetic and distinctive without the intensity of red or the aggression of neon. It differentiates immediately from the blue/orange of every other cycling app.
3. **"Consumer polish, not sports-tool grit."** — Closer to Duolingo or Calm in approachability, closer to Linear in UI cleanliness. No aggressive angles, no neon gradients, no stock imagery of pro cyclists in full kit on mountain passes.
4. **"Real people. Real bikes. Real moments."** — Photography and illustration should show someone cycling to work, a weekend ride with a friend, checking a phone mid-ride. Diversity by default, not as a statement. The visual language says "this is for you" to someone who has never used a training app before.
5. **"Warmth in every interaction, clarity in every screen."** — Every button, label, and message should feel considered. Microinteractions matter. The app should feel like something people want to open, not a tool they tolerate using.
